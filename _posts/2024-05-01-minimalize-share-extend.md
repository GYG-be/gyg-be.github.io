# Open source community as an educational institution

In order to really participate in a collaborative community development effort, you really need to role up your sleeves and *go to school* ... yes, of course, it's about writing code ... but it's WAY MORE than just writing code by yourself ... it's about writing code in a meaningful way with others AND NONE ARE YOU ARE GETTING PAID ... and nobody has to be there -- EVERYBODY involved has to be doing it for the motivation of the community ... and discerning the motivation of the community is a whole different ballgame than you doing something by yourself for your own private motivations ...

In a nutshell, if you want to participate, it's totally ON YOU to learn all of the ins/outs of the shared parts of development CI/CD toolchain that everyone relies upon in order to participate ... it's totally on you to autodidactly learn the pre-requisites of collaborative development ... AND you will need to learn the groups motivations and everyone needs to have a somewhat shared roadmap or vision of where things are going and what the priorities are for getting there ... AND you will need to learn how to sustain the motivations of other group members [without writing them a check] ... doing all of these things WELL is more important [and generally tougher]than any college education [through PhD level]. In other words, ***do not underestimate the difficulty ... don't expect it to be easy.***

# How to START getting involved

Maybe for the starting fundamentals of fundamentals ...  we should look a lot more closely at free and open source licenses ... *so that's a big* ***TBD*** right off the bat ...

In general, as a very hand-wavee summation of what open source licenses allude to, perhaps if you read between the lines, the dues that you implicitly agree to pay for using FREE open source software are about the expectation that you will use it and through your use you will START to become part of the dev community ... at first, you are still outside of the dev community, but you are USING the software with the intention of maybe becoming familiar with ... as you become a POWER user of the software, you inevitablity will notice things that could be tweak or tuned up ... that's when you might post a remark or question or something that might lead to an issue to be worked on ... you might even get work on the issue [perhaps with someone else familiar with the whole codebase] and possibly refactor the code in your own fork of the code ... if your little innovation really works, you will share your attempts and work on your own approaches to encourage sharing, you will generally improve the community and possibly even the software as your own needs change you will fork and extend the idea.
